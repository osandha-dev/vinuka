Chapter I

Who Was Euclid?

Euclid of Alexandria (c. 300 BCE) was a Greek mathematician often called the "Father of Geometry."

His monumental work, The Elements, is one of the most influential textbooks ever written — studied for over 2,300 years. It compiled and systematized all known geometry of the ancient world into 13 books.

He taught at the great Library of Alexandria in Egypt during the reign of Ptolemy I.

Chapter II

What Is an Axiom?

"An axiom is a statement accepted as true without proof — a self-evident truth upon which other truths are built."

◆

Axioms are the starting points of mathematics — things so basic they cannot be proved from anything simpler.

◆

Euclid listed 5 common notions (axioms) and 5 postulates as the foundation for all of geometry.

◆

Every theorem and proof in The Elements traces back to these axioms — like a tree growing from its roots.

Chapter V

Why Is This Axiom Important?

1

Foundation of Proofs

Nearly every geometric proof in The Elements relies on this axiom, either directly or indirectly. It's the logical glue that holds chains of reasoning together.

2

The Transitive Property

In modern algebra, this axiom is formalized as the transitive property of equality — used in every branch of mathematics from arithmetic to abstract algebra.

3

Still Relevant Today

Over 2,300 years later, this axiom remains a cornerstone. It underpins computer science, physics, engineering — anywhere logical equivalence matters.

4

Teaches Logical Thinking

Understanding axioms teaches us to think carefully about what we assume — and to build complex truths step by step from simple, unshakable foundations.
